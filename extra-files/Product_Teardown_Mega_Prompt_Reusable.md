## THE PROMPT:

You are a Senior Product Manager writing a detailed product teardown for your personal portfolio website. The teardown should demonstrate your product thinking to recruiters at top-tier technology companies (Google, Microsoft, Apple, Amazon, Meta).

**Product to Teardown:** [PRODUCT NAME — e.g., "Spotify", "Notion", "Uber Eats", "Microsoft Copilot for Power BI"]

**Target Company I'm Applying To:** [COMPANY NAME — e.g., "Google", "Microsoft", "SSE"]

**My Background (for personal lens):** [YOUR BRIEF BIO — e.g., "Product Owner at a UK bank's AI Centre of Excellence, previously shipped AR at Dyson and contributed to PS5 launch at Sony. GCP certified. Based in Bristol, UK."]

**My Relevant Experience:** [ANY PERSONAL EXPERIENCE WITH THE PRODUCT OR SIMILAR PRODUCTS — e.g., "I've used this product daily for 2 years" or "I've built a competing internal tool at my company"]

---

Please write a complete, publication-ready product teardown structured EXACTLY as follows. Every section must be detailed and substantive — no filler. Use specific numbers, real data points, and concrete analysis. The teardown should be 4,000–6,000 words.

### REQUIRED STRUCTURE:

**1. Executive Summary (150 words)**
- What the product is, key stats, and what this teardown will cover
- End with a sentence about the 3 features you'll propose

**2. Product Overview (300-400 words)**
- What the product actually is (go deeper than the marketing description)
- Identify which product categories it straddles (most great products sit at the intersection of 2-3 categories)
- Key metrics table: users, revenue, market share, growth rate — cite sources
- Describe the core value loop/flywheel

**3. Competitive Landscape (400-500 words)**
- Market position table: product vs top 3-4 competitors with metrics
- Competitive moat analysis: identify 3-5 reinforcing moat layers
- Where competitors genuinely have advantages (be intellectually honest — one-sided analysis is weak)

**4. Monetisation Strategy Deep Dive (300-400 words)**
- Break down each revenue stream with estimated contribution percentages
- Explain WHY each stream works (what user behaviour enables it)
- Draw the monetisation flywheel as an ASCII diagram showing how revenue streams reinforce each other
- Identify any monetisation gaps or missed opportunities

**5. Key Ecosystem/Community Analysis (300-400 words)**
- If the product has a UGC/community component, analyse it (e.g., Local Guides for Maps, creators for YouTube, hosts for Airbnb)
- What incentive structures exist? What's the gamification model?
- Where does the incentive model break down?
- What would happen if 10% of top contributors left?

**6. Recent Product Strategy (300 words)**
- What are the most recent 2-3 major product launches/updates?
- Analyse each through a PM lens: why was it built, what problem does it solve, what was the strategic motivation?
- Give your honest assessment: was this the right move?

**7. User Journey Mapping (300 words)**
- Map 2 key user journeys in detail using ASCII flow diagrams
- Journey 1: The primary use case (e.g., daily/weekly usage)
- Journey 2: A secondary use case (e.g., discovery, onboarding, or power-user flow)
- Identify monetisation touchpoints, decision points, and drop-off risks

**8. Three Feature Proposals (800-1000 words EACH — this is the centrepiece)**

For EACH of the 3 features, include ALL of the following:

a) **The Problem** (100 words): What specific user pain point or business gap exists? Use data to validate the problem.

b) **The Solution** (150 words): Describe the feature clearly. What does the user see and do?

c) **Wireframe** (ASCII art): Create a detailed ASCII wireframe showing the key screens/interface. Make it specific enough that a designer could understand the intent.

d) **User Flow** (if different from wireframe): Show the step-by-step flow from trigger to completion.

e) **Monetisation Potential** (50 words): How does this feature generate or protect revenue?

f) **Success Metrics** (50 words): Primary metric, secondary metric, and guardrail metric.

g) **RICE Score Table**: Score each factor (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort) out of 10 with reasoning, then calculate the RICE score.

**IMPORTANT for Feature Proposals:**
- At least one feature should directly relate to my target company's product strategy
- At least one feature should address an underserved user segment
- At least one feature should leverage a technology trend (AI, AR, IoT, etc.)
- Draw from my personal experience/background where relevant

**9. Prioritisation Summary (100 words)**
- Table ranking the 3 features by RICE score
- Recommended build order with rationale (which may differ from pure RICE)

**10. Risks & Open Questions (150 words)**
- For each feature, identify the biggest risk and one open question
- Show you think about failure modes, not just happy paths

**11. What This Teardown Demonstrates (100 words)**
- Explicitly connect this analysis to 4-5 PM competencies
- Frame it so a recruiter scanning quickly understands what skills you're showing

**12. Author Bio (50 words)**
- Professional bio with relevant credentials
- Links to portfolio and LinkedIn

### STYLE & FORMATTING RULES:

- Write in first person ("I", "my analysis") — this is a personal portfolio piece, not a corporate report
- Use British English spelling (analyse, monetisation, organisation, etc.)
- Be opinionated — "I believe," "my assessment is" — avoid bland neutrality
- Include specific data points with source attribution
- Use markdown formatting (headers, tables, bold) for scannability
- ASCII diagrams should be clean and well-formatted
- Every claim should be supported by either data or explicit reasoning
- The tone should be: confident, analytical, occasionally witty, never arrogant
- Total length: 5,000-7,000 words

---

### WHAT MAKES A GREAT TEARDOWN (internal checklist):

✅ Goes beyond surface-level observation to structural analysis
✅ Includes real numbers and data, not vague claims
✅ Shows empathy for multiple user segments
✅ Feature proposals are specific enough to build, not just ideas
✅ RICE scoring forces prioritisation discipline
✅ Acknowledges weaknesses and trade-offs (intellectual honesty)
✅ Connects back to the author's personal experience
✅ A recruiter at the target company would learn something new reading this
✅ The wireframes are detailed enough that a designer could interpret them
✅ The monetisation analysis shows business thinking, not just product thinking
